b) Is your observed signal biased? (e.g. is the observed value equally likely to be stronger or weaker than the true injected signal?) Is it symmetric?¶

I think it is biased. The curve of the distribution itself is not symmetric, because it follows a lognormal distribution (which is not symmetric like a Gaussian). This means it's biased for values greater than zero (that's the shape of the lognorm- where the 'peak' is tells us about how likely values are to fall in that part of the distribution).

d) For your observed signal, what is the 1𝜎 uncertainty on the true signal strength?¶

This is basically integration. We don't have the cdf of this distribution, so we do it "manually", then equate it to a normal distribution sigma, and get the uncertainty.

In [16]:
point = Psd[250]
bins = np.linspace(signal_strengths.min(), signal_strengths.max(),50_000)

partial_sums = np.cumsum(point) / np.sum(point)
index = np.argmin(abs(partial_sums - stats.norm.cdf(-1)))
mariaiscool=bins[index - 1]
uncertainty = mariaiscool - bins[np.argmax(point)]

print(f"The 1 sigma uncertainty is {uncertainty}")

The 1 sigma uncertainty is -14668.336378614884

e) Discuss the answer to part d in some depth. Is it symmetric? Is it biased? Does this make sense?¶

The uncertainty we get is in one direction only- this has to do with the fact that our lognormal distribution is not symmetric, and also biased to one side. This is why we have the one sigma - uncertainty, but not a sigma + uncertainty. This makes sense based on what I said before.
